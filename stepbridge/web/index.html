Step Ribbon — Editing Gestures

The ribbon is always live — playhead and gate animations continue while you edit. The help bar beneath each track shows these shortcuts at a glance.

Colour & Symbol Key

The same key appears at the bottom of the editor. Each step cell in the pitch ribbon is colour-coded by its state; the two thin strips below each cell show gate length and probability independently.
SymbolMeaning
Blue bar A normal sounding step. Height of the bar reflects the pitch — taller = higher note, shorter = lower note.
Teal bar Tied step — extends the gate of the preceding step rather than retriggering. The teal cell has no gate strip of its own because its gate is controlled by the originating step.
Blue-red bar Accented step — MIDI note-on is sent at higher velocity (127 vs ~96). On CV/Pulse tracks the pulse output is unchanged (Pulse is binary); accent is a MIDI-only effect.
Empty cell Rest — no gate, no MIDI note-on. CV holds its last value through the rest.
×NAmber badge Ratchet count — the step fires N times within its slot, each at 1/N of the step duration. Ratchet and tie are mutually exclusive. Visible on notes with ratchetCount ≥ 2.
Green strip Gate length strip — the thin bar below each pitch cell. Fill proportion = gate percentage (1–100%). A full-width strip = 100% gate (legato); a narrow sliver = short staccato gate.
Amber strip Probability strip — the thin bar below the gate strip. Fill = step probability (0–7 mapped to 0–100%). A full strip means the step always fires (level 7); shorter strips mean the step fires on a random fraction of playback cycles — level 4 ≈ 57%, level 1 ≈ 14%.

Track Footer — Timing Tab

The track footer is tabbed: Timing · Playback · Generative · Chord · Pitch · MIDI · Output. The active tab persists across state refreshes so it does not reset while editing.
ControlEffect
LengthNumber of active steps (1–64). Steps beyond the current length are hidden but not erased — regrowing the track reveals the unchanged data. Scrollable.
Bar (time signature)Steps per bar group in Regular mode. Controls where bar-divider lines appear on the ribbon and where the Randomize algorithm places accents. Scrollable. For non-standard meters, switch to Irregular mode (described below).
Irregular modeWhen enabled, the time signature becomes an additive group sequence (e.g. 3+3+2 for a 3+3+2 meter). Enter the group sizes in the chips editor. The sequence repeats across the full track length; the final group is truncated if it extends past the end. Randomize and Global Randomize use the irregular bar boundaries when weighting accent positions.

Track Footer — Playback Tab

Arpeggiator

The arpeggiator reorders step playback by pitch rather than position, producing chord-like patterns from the notes already written in the steps. The stored note data is never changed — only the playback order is affected. Rest steps participate as silence in the sequence (or as arp positions when Include Rests is enabled).
ModePlayback order
OffSteps play in their written position order (default behaviour).
ForwardAll pitched steps sorted lowest to highest note, looping continuously. Identical to playing a rising arpeggio.
ReverseAll pitched steps sorted highest to lowest note, looping continuously.
PingPongAscending then descending, endpoints sounding once each: lowest → highest → second-highest → … → second-lowest → lowest → … The turnaround is seamless — no repeated endpoint. For two notes, alternates between them.
RandomA new random permutation of pitched steps on each cycle. Different every repeat, never predictable.
ConvergePairs the extreme notes inward: lowest, highest, second-lowest, second-highest, third-lowest, … until reaching the middle. On an odd number of steps, the single centre note closes the sequence.
DivergeThe mirror of Converge: starts at the middle and expands outward toward the extremes. On an odd number of steps, the single centre note opens the sequence.
Include Rests (Converge and Diverge only): when enabled, rest steps are appended as silent positions after the pitched sequence, so the arp pattern plays its notes then rests before repeating. This adds rhythmic space and makes the pattern length match the full track length instead of only the pitched-step count.
The arpeggiator order is pre-computed whenever notes or track length change, so there is no CPU cost at audio rate. Playhead position in the arp resets to the beginning whenever the transport resets to step 0.

Track Footer — Output Tab

Glide (Portamento)

Glide is CV-only — MIDI note output always sends discrete integer pitches. Rest steps do not retrigger the slide; the CV holds its last value through rests.
ControlEffect
Glide sliderCV pitch sweep time in milliseconds (0 = off, instant pitch change). When non-zero the CV voltage slides continuously from the previous pitch to the new one over the specified time.
Max (range knob)Sets the upper end of the glide slider range. The range is remembered per track across state refreshes.

Accent CV Output (CV tracks only)

Sends a signal on the Audio L jack (Track 1) or Audio R jack (Track 2) each time an accented step fires. The audio outputs are AC-coupled, so a sustained DC signal decays toward 0V at the receiving module — use Spike or Hybrid for reliable transient shapes. Gate mode can work for fast envelopes that respond to the rising edge only.
ModeSignal shape
OffNo signal output (default). Audio jacks carry no signal from StepBridge.
SpikeFull-scale burst lasting 5 ms at the moment an accented gate opens. The AC coupling naturally produces a sharp positive transient at the destination — ideal for pinging a filter, envelope, or slew with a short impulse.
GateFull-scale signal for the entire gate duration of accented steps. The AC coupling causes the signal to droop exponentially back toward 0V during the gate — the time constant depends on the receiving module's input impedance. Useful when the receiver responds to the rising edge.
HybridSpike (5 ms at full scale) followed by a sustained half-level signal for the remainder of the gate. Combines the sharp transient of Spike with a brief hold that can sustain an envelope or VCA through the accented note.
Non-accent steps output 0 V in all modes, so the signal is fully gated. Muted tracks produce no accent output.

Randomize — Single Track

The Randomize button in the track header uses a Markov chain stochastic model inspired by Xenakis's compositional techniques. All parameters are generated in a single firmware-side pass and respect the track's key, scale, and bar structure.
ParameterAlgorithm
PitchMarkov chain over scale degrees. Transition weights follow an exponential decay with distance: moving to an adjacent degree is twice as likely as skipping one, four times as likely as skipping two, and so on. Degree 0 (the tonic) receives extra weight on every transition — a gravitational pull that keeps the melody from drifting away from home. Self-transitions (same note repeated) are allowed but rare. The chain starts at a random degree in octave 4–5 and self-corrects if it drifts outside the C2–C6 register.
Rests~20% per step, reduced to ~8% on bar-starts so metric downbeats are usually sounding. Determined before the pitch draw so pitch state only advances for active steps.
Accent~65% on bar-starts, ~12% elsewhere. Bar position is determined by the track's own time signature (Regular or Irregular).
Gate lengthExponential distribution: ~39% short (10–30%), ~39% medium (35–70%), ~22% long (75–95%). More short gates than long, giving a naturally articulated feel rather than uniform legato.
Ratchet / tiePoisson-inspired: ~79% single note, ~8% double ratchet, ~5% triple, ~1% quadruple, ~7% tie to previous step. Tie and ratchet are mutually exclusive — setting one always clears the other.
Step probabilityEach step is assigned a probability level (1–7) drawn from a skewed distribution: ~55% always-fire (7), with decreasing probability of lower values down to ~2% never-fire (1). Steps with partial probability create variation on every playback cycle — the pattern sounds slightly different each time around.

Randomize — Global (⚄ All Tracks)

Global Randomize runs a single coordinated generation pass across all tracks, creating complementary rhythmic roles rather than independent random patterns. This models the "interplay and bouncing" of ensemble parts rather than tracks that ignore each other.

Style (Melodic / Intervallic / Floating) and structure flags (vary len / vary meter) are taken from the toolbar controls — the per-track Generative tab settings have no effect on Global Randomize. Use the per-track Randomize button if you want each track to use its own style and structure preferences independently.
AspectHow it works
Track rolesEven-numbered tracks (T1, T3) are assigned the "primary" role — active more often on beat positions and bar-starts. Odd-numbered tracks (T2, T4) are assigned the "complement" role — active more often on off-beats and between primary activity. Both roles are active on bar-starts so the downbeat feels grounded.
Time signature awarenessEach track's own time signature (Regular or Irregular) is used independently to classify each of its steps as a bar-start, on-beat, or off-beat. A T1 in 7/8 and a T2 in 4/4 each generate rhythms shaped by their own meter — the roles interact without forcing a shared grid.
Rest probabilitiesPrimary tracks: ~8% rest on bar-starts, ~20% on beats, ~48% on off-beats. Complement tracks: ~8% on bar-starts, ~52% on beats, ~18% on off-beats. The overlap on bar-starts creates tutti anchor points.
Gate length contrastPrimary tracks receive the same gate distribution as single-track Randomize (more legato). Complement tracks are biased shorter (~38% very short 8–25%, ~22% medium 28–55%, ~40% longer 58–80%), making them more staccato and percussive against the primary.
Step probabilityProbabilities are skewed significantly lower than in single-track Randomize (~38% always-fire vs ~55%). More steps carry partial probability values (1–6), so over successive playback cycles tracks independently choose to fire or stay silent — the inter-track texture keeps shifting, creating the "bouncing" effect where activity seems to pass between tracks over time.
PitchSame Markov chain as single-track Randomize. Each track runs an independent chain starting from a different point in the PRNG sequence, so pitch content is distinct across tracks even though the rhythmic structure is coordinated.
UndoPer-track snapshots are captured before generation, so each track's ↩ Undo button works independently after a Global Randomize — you can undo just T2 while keeping T1, T3, T4.

Mutation — Stockhausen Drift

Inspired by Karlheinz Stockhausen's indeterminate compositional techniques: individual note pitches drift slowly over time through small random increments, so the pattern gradually evolves away from where it started. Unlike Randomize (which generates a new pattern in one pass), Mutation is a continuous process — the pattern changes are imperceptible moment to moment but accumulate into something genuinely different over bars and minutes.

Mutation is an overlay: the stored pattern is never modified while mutation is running. You hear the drifted notes, but the underlying steps remain unchanged. If you hear something you want to keep, click ⤓ Latch to write the current drifted pitches permanently into the pattern. Save-to-slot always captures whatever is currently stored — so Latch first, then Save if you want to preserve a mutated state.
ParameterDetail
Rate (4–512 steps)How often the mutation engine fires a tick. At 4 steps the pattern evolves quickly — a 16-step loop at 120 BPM will tick four times per bar. At 512 steps it can take many minutes of playback before any note noticeably shifts. Rate counts this track's own step advances, so tracks with different lengths drift at different absolute rates even at the same Rate setting — shorter tracks tick more often per clock second.
Depth (subtle → max)Controls two things simultaneously: the probability that a given step is affected on each tick (~12% at subtle, ~100% at max), and the maximum accumulated semitone delta (±2 at subtle, ±9 at max). Low depth gives sparse, cautious drift where only occasional notes shift by one or two semitones. High depth can eventually push every note to the edge of its allowed range, producing a pattern that has moved substantially from the original.
In ScaleWhen checked, the effective (drifted) note is snapped to the nearest note in the track's current Draw Scale key and scale at output time. The semitone delta still accumulates internally — notes only audibly shift when the delta is large enough to pull them to the next scale degree. In C Major, a C will stay silent until the delta reaches ≥1 semitone above C, at which point it snaps to D (2 semitones up); at ≥3 semitones it snaps to E, and so on. This creates a "threshold drift" effect — the drift still accumulates continuously, but the output feels quantised to the scale. Latch in this mode writes the snapped pitches, not the raw deltas. Uncheck for fully chromatic drift regardless of scale.
Chromatic scaleWhen the track's scale is set to Chromatic, the In Scale option has no effect — every semitone is by definition in scale. Mutation on a Chromatic track is always fully chromatic regardless of the checkbox. This is the most Stockhausen-faithful mode: atonal, freely wandering pitch material.
Rest stepsRests are never mutated. A step set to rest stays a rest — mutation only drifts the pitch of sounding notes.
⤓ LatchCommits the current overlay: writes each step's effective (drifted, and scale-snapped if In Scale is active) pitch permanently into the stored pattern, then clears all deltas to zero. The mutation engine continues running from this new baseline. Use this to capture a happy accident before saving. Note that Latch is irreversible — there is no undo for it (though loading a previously saved slot will restore an earlier state).
On/off behaviourDisabling mutation clears all accumulated deltas immediately — the pattern snaps back to its stored pitches. Re-enabling starts a fresh drift from zero. The rate and depth settings are preserved across on/off cycles.
Interaction with RandomizeRandomize writes new pitches to the stored pattern and also resets the undo snapshot. If mutation is running when you Randomize, the mutation deltas remain but now apply on top of the newly randomized pattern. This can produce interesting compound textures — a randomized structural backbone that slowly drifts in character. If you don't want that, disable mutation before randomizing, or clear it manually with the on/off toggle.

Phase Setup (⇌ Phase)

Brian Eno's tape-phasing and "Music for Airports" technique: multiple loops of different lengths play simultaneously. Because the lengths are coprime (share no common factor), the loops drift continuously in and out of phase — the combination of patterns keeps shifting without ever repeating exactly until the LCM (Least Common Multiple) of all lengths has elapsed.
ConceptDetail
Coprime lengthsTwo numbers are coprime if their only common factor is 1 (GCD = 1), meaning their LCM equals their product. For example, 7 and 11 are coprime: LCM = 77, so the combined pattern takes 77 steps to fully repeat. By contrast, 8 and 12 share factor 4, giving LCM = 24 — a much shorter cycle.
LCM and complexityA higher LCM means a longer time before all tracks re-align. T1=7 + T2=11 (LCM 77) sounds like a short, evolving loop. T1=11 + T2=13 (LCM 143) takes nearly twice as long. Three coprime tracks multiply the complexity dramatically: 7+11+13 gives LCM 1001.
Applying a presetThe Phase panel shows presets appropriate for your current track count, sorted by LCM from simplest to most complex. Clicking Apply sends SET_LENGTH to each track and closes the panel. Track content is unchanged — only lengths are set.
Reset alignmentUse ▶ from start to reset all playheads to step 0, re-synchronising the tracks to hear the pattern from the beginning of its full LCM cycle.
With other featuresPhase presets combine naturally with the Arpeggiator, Randomize, and Global Randomize. A good workflow: set coprime lengths via Phase Setup, then Global Randomize to fill all tracks — the complementary rhythmic roles will drift in and out of alignment as the loops cycle.
Preset examples (2 tracks): 7+11 (LCM 77) · 7+13 (LCM 91) · 11+13 (LCM 143) · 8+11 (LCM 88) · 8+9 (LCM 72) · 12+11 (LCM 132)
3 tracks: 7+11+13 (LCM 1001) · 8+11+13 (LCM 1144) · 7+8+11 (LCM 616)
4 tracks: 7+11+13+16 (LCM 16016) · 7+11+13+17 (LCM 17017)
